A Different Way To Look At Dark Matter

March 22, 2026

What If Dark Matter Isn’t a What… But a Who?

Published on CosmicHeist

In the last article, we followed one of the most violent events imaginable — the beginning of the universe. A kind of cosmic “shockwave” expanding outward, carrying energy, matter, and the seeds of everything we see today. Galaxies. Stars. Planets. Us. But there was something strange hiding in that picture. Something we didn’t really talk about. Because even in that early, chaotic universe… most of what was there wasn’t visible at all.

The Part We Can’t See

When we look at the universe today, everything that shines — every star, every galaxy — only makes up a tiny fraction of what’s actually out there. Roughly 5%. The rest? It doesn’t glow. It doesn’t reflect. It doesn’t interact with light in any meaningful way. And yet, it’s there. Holding galaxies together. Shaping the structure of the universe. Quietly dominating everything. We call it dark matter. Whlich is just another way of saying: we don’t know what this is.

The Question We Keep Asking

So naturally, we ask: What is dark matter made of? Is it a new particle? Something heavier? Slower? Stranger? Physicists have ideas — WIMPs, axions, exotic particles that barely interact with anything. And maybe one of those is right. But there’s something about the question itself that feels… incomplete.

A Slightly Different Angle

Let me phrase it differently. Up until now, we’ve been treating dark matter like it’s just another ingredient in the universe. Like hydrogen. Like dust. Like radiation. Something passive. Something that just exists. But what if that assumption is doing more work than we realise? What if instead of asking: What is dark matter… we asked: Who is dark matter?

That Sounds Crazy… Right?

Yeah, it does. And to be clear — there’s no evidence that dark matter is conscious, alive, or anything like that. This isn’t about making a claim. It’s about noticing something subtle. Because the moment you switch from what to who, the entire frame changes. You stop thinking in terms of: particles substances simple ingredients And you start thinking in terms of: structure organisation systems we don’t yet understand

The Invisible Framework

Here’s the part that really gets me. Current theories suggest that dark matter forms a kind of cosmic scaffolding. A massive, invisible framework that galaxies form inside of. Not around. Not randomly. Inside. Which means all the visible matter — stars, planets, everything we’ve ever observed — might just be: A thin, glowing layer sitting on top of something much larger. Something we can’t see. Something we can barely detect. Something that was already shaping the universe right from the beginning.

Back to the Beginning

Think back to that early universe again. The expansion. The energy. The formation of the first structures. We tend to imagine it as a kind of chaotic explosion that gradually settles into order. But what if there was already a hidden structure guiding that process? Not in a mystical sense. Just in a way we don’t yet fully understand. Something that doesn’t interact with light, but still influences everything that does.

A Thought We Usually Avoid

There’s a line we don’t like to cross in science. The line between: matter and meaning Between what exists physically… and what we feel might exist beyond that. We call things “non-physical” when we don’t know how to measure them. We call them “abstract.” “Metaphysical.” Sometimes even “soul.”But here’s the uncomfortable question: What if “non-physical” is just a placeholder for something we don’t yet know how to detect? If something could exist… without interacting with light… yet still shape the universe… would we recognise it? Or would we simply give it a name and move on?

Maybe We’re Asking Beginner Questions

Here’s the uncomfortable thought. Maybe the problem isn’t just that we don’t know what dark matter is. Maybe we’re still asking the kind of questions you ask when you’ve only just started looking. Science has done this before. We asked what light is — and got waves, then particles, then something in between. We asked what space is — and discovered spacetime. We asked what matter is — and found fields, probabilities, uncertainty. Each time, the answer wasn’t just new. The question itself had to evolve.

So… What Are We Missing?

I’m not saying dark matter is a “who” in the literal sense. But I am saying this: If 95% of the universe is unknown, then our assumptions about it are probably wrong in ways we don’t yet see. And sometimes, the easiest way to expose those assumptions is to ask a slightly strange question. A question that doesn’t quite fit.

Final thought

The universe we see might not be the full story. It might not even be the main story. And somewhere in that invisible majority, there may be patterns, structures — maybe even entire layers of reality — that we’re only just beginning to sense. So maybe the real question isn’t: What is dark matter? Maybe it’s: What kind of question is dark matter forcing us to ask?
